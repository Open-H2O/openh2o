The short version

When a district delivers canal water to its farms, two questions decide how the platform books that water. First: of the water that reaches a field, how much does the crop actually drink, and how much soaks back down into the aquifer? Second: if a district doesn’t use all the surface water it was entitled to in a year, does the leftover carry into next year or disappear? You answer both once, on the Delivery Settings page, and they apply to every district.

Setting 1 — how much of a delivery the crop uses

Not every drop delivered to a field is “used up.” A crop drinks part of the water; the rest sinks past the roots and recharges the aquifer underneath. This setting is that share — the percentage of delivered water the crop actually consumes. A typical figure is 75%, meaning three-quarters is taken up by the plants and the remaining quarter returns to the groundwater as recharge.

Why it matters: the platform splits a district’s total monthly delivery across its farms by how thirsty each crop is that month. A field growing a thirsty summer crop pulls a larger share than a fallow or low-water field next door. This percentage caps how much any one field can be credited with using, so a delivery is shared sensibly instead of split evenly regardless of what’s growing.

Setting 2 — what happens to unused water at year-end

A district has a surface-water allotment for the water year. If it doesn’t use the whole thing by the time the year closes, you choose what the platform does with the leftover:

  • Carry it forward as a credit toward next year. The unused amount opens next year as extra water the district can draw on. This is the default.
  • Let it expire (use-it-or-lose-it). The leftover simply disappears at year-end; next year starts fresh with no bonus.

One thing that doesn’t change either way: if a district overdraws — uses more than its allotment — that shortfall always carries into next year as an obligation. An overdraw is a real debt; only a surplus is affected by this choice.

Who sets these — and when

These are agency policy, not farmer entry. An analyst or administrator sets them once during setup and revisits them rarely — for example, if the agency adopts a different efficiency assumption, or its board changes the year-end rule. A farmer never touches them. Because they steer every future delivery calculation, change them deliberately, the same way you would the methodology.

When one district needs to differ

The year-end rule is set agency-wide, but a single district can be flipped to a different choice without changing everyone else. Open that district’s page and use the Year-end unused water control: leave it on “Use agency default” for the common case, or set it to carry-forward or expire just for that district. Switching it back to the agency default means that district will follow along automatically if you later change the agency-wide rule.
