The short version

Turning a satellite measurement into a number you can stand behind is a chain of subtractions. Start from what the crops actually used — the one thing we can measure for every field, from space. Then take away what rain covered, and what the canals delivered. Whatever use is still left unexplained is, on a field with a well, the groundwater figure.

No single clever formula — just honest bookkeeping, one supply at a time. If “measured use vs. supplies” is new, start with How Water Balances Work; this page picks up where that one leaves off and walks the actual arithmetic.

Step by step

Every field runs through the same ordered chain. Each step removes one supply we can account for — and the order matters: you subtract the free water (rain) and the delivered water (canals) before asking what groundwater had to make up the difference.

1
Start with gross ET

The total water the crops consumed that month, measured from satellites — evapotranspiration. This is the demand the field had to meet, whatever the source.

2
Subtract effective precipitation

Rain that actually soaked in and was available to the crop — not every drop that fell, just the share the plants could use. That much demand was met for free.

3
Subtract surface-water deliveries

Canal water the district delivered to the field. What the crop drank from that delivery comes off the demand next.

4
Subtract edge cases

Optional adjustments an agency may configure — a floor that prevents a negative result, banked wet-month surplus, and the like. Most fields never need these.

5
What remains is billable groundwater the result

But only on a field that has a well. The platform writes that leftover as a groundwater figure where a well exists, and as unmet demand (the crop wanted more than it got) where one doesn’t. It never invents pumping out of a missing meter.

This chain isn’t hard-coded — an agency tunes it on the Methodology Settings page, where these exact steps appear as a reorderable, switch-on/switch-off list. The help text here and that settings screen describe the same chain, so what you read matches what you configure. Because every future calculation flows through it, change it deliberately.

Not all rain counts. “Effective” precipitation is the portion of rainfall the crop could actually use — what soaked into the root zone and stayed there. Rain that ran off, or fell faster than the soil could absorb, or landed on already-saturated ground doesn’t reduce the crop’s draw on other supplies, so it isn’t subtracted. Agencies pick how to estimate the effective share: a flat fraction of total rain, or a soil-storage model (the USDA-SCS / TR-21 method), tuned on the Methodology Settings page.

The chain is a step-walker, not a single equation. Under the hood it’s a small configurable engine (evaluate_chain) that runs whatever ordered steps the agency has enabled. Crucially, it’s source-agnostic: it reads measured consumptive use for any parcel and works through the supplies, with no well required to run. A well only matters at the very end — it decides whether the unexplained leftover is booked as groundwater or recorded as unmet demand.

Sharing one delivery across many fields

Here is the problem that makes this platform necessary. A water district holds one water right and delivers canal water to dozens of farms — but the canal isn’t metered field by field. All the district knows is the total it sent down the ditch. So how much of that total belongs to each farm?

Split it evenly the wrong way

Every field gets the same share, no matter what’s growing. A fallow field is credited the same as a thirsty vineyard — so water lands on fields that never used it, and the fields that did go short.

Split by ET demand how it works

Each field’s share tracks what its crop actually drank that month. The thirsty summer crop draws the larger share; the fallow field draws almost none. Each share is also capped, so no field is credited with using more than it physically could.

Read the Surface Delivery Settings → {% include "partials/_deep_dive.html" with label="The measurement-first ladder and where the cap comes from" body="

When the platform has to choose a number, it follows a strict priority ladder — measurement first, estimate last:

  1. A per-field direct measurement wins over everything — if someone metered this field, that’s the truth.
  2. A district’s hand-set value is next — an agency’s own recorded figure beats any estimate.
  3. Satellite ET fills the gap only when nothing was measured or entered.
  4. An even split is the last resort, used only when there’s no better signal at all.

A district’s recorded total is always honored as-is; ET only decides how to split the unmeasured per-field breakdown of that total. The cap on each field’s share is its demand divided by an efficiency factor — because not all delivered water is consumed (some sinks past the roots and recharges the aquifer). That efficiency, typically around 75%, is an agency setting; the Surface Delivery Settings page explains it in plain language.

Sharing a well or a headgate

The same logic settles a smaller, thornier case: when two or more fields share a single well or a single headgate, and only the combined pumping or diversion is known. The old shortcut was to split it 50/50 (or evenly among however many fields). That’s the same mistake as the canal — it ignores what was actually growing.

By demand, not by headcount

A shared well serving a vineyard and an alfalfa field is apportioned by each field’s measured ET demand. If the vineyard consumed twice what the alfalfa did that month, it carries twice the share of the pumping. The total always reconciles back to what the well or headgate actually produced — the demand weights only decide how that known total is divided.
