The short version

A handful of settings shape how every number on this platform is calculated. They’re agency policy, not daily data entry — an analyst or administrator sets them once during setup and revisits them rarely. A farmer never touches them. This page is the reference for each one: what it controls, who owns it, and when a change is actually warranted. The deeper detail sits under each “Advanced” toggle for when you need it.

The methodology / calculation plan

This is the ordered chain of subtractions the platform runs on every field — gross ET, minus effective rain, minus surface deliveries, minus any edge cases — to arrive at a billable groundwater figure. You can reorder the steps, switch any of them off, or tune how a step behaves (for example, how “effective” rainfall is estimated).

Who sets it: an administrator, on the Methodology Settings page.
When to change it: rarely, and deliberately. Every calculation the platform produces flows through this chain, so a change re-shapes past and future numbers alike. Treat it like amending a policy, not flipping a switch — the step-by-step walkthrough is on Methods Behind the Numbers.

Each step in the chain has its own knobs. The two with the most options:

  • Effective precipitation — choose how to estimate the share of rain the crop could use: a flat fraction of total rainfall, or a soil-storage model (USDA-SCS / TR-21). This decides how much rain is credited before groundwater is.
  • Floor / banking (an edge-case step) — a floor keeps a result from going negative, and an optional bank carries a wet-month surplus forward against later dry months. Most agencies leave these at their defaults.

The order matters: the platform subtracts free water (rain) and delivered water (canals) before asking what groundwater had to make up the difference. Reordering changes which supply gets “credited” first.

Surface delivery settings

Two agency-wide settings govern how canal deliveries are counted: how much of a delivery the crop actually consumes (the rest sinks past the roots and recharges the aquifer — typically around 75%), and what happens to a district’s unused water at year-end (carry it forward as a credit, or let it expire). Together they decide how a district’s total monthly delivery is split across its farms and what a leftover allotment is worth.

Who sets it: the analyst, on the Delivery Settings page.
When to change it: when the agency adopts a different efficiency assumption, or its board changes the year-end rule.

These two have their own dedicated walkthrough, so this page won’t repeat it. Read the full Surface Delivery Settings page →

Recovery horizon

This is the per-district control over that year-end question: when a district doesn’t use its full surface-water allotment, does the leftover carry forward as a credit it can draw on next year, or expire at year-end (use-it-or-lose-it)? The agency sets one default for everyone on the Delivery Settings page; the recovery horizon lets any single district depart from that default without affecting the rest.

Who sets it: an analyst, on an individual district’s page (under Zones), using the “Year-end unused water” control.
When to change it: when one district’s rules genuinely differ — otherwise leave it on “Use agency default,” so a later agency-wide change flows through automatically.

One thing this setting never changes: an overdraw — using more than the allotment — always carries forward as a debt. Only a surplus is governed by the carry-forward-or-expire choice.

Access control

One system-level switch decides whether the platform’s front door is open or locked. For this demonstration it is deliberately off — the front door is open so anyone can look around. Flipping it on is the “go-live” step a real agency takes when it’s ready to restrict the platform to its own staff, and it’s intentionally deferred until the demo has been evaluated. Most readers never need to touch this; the detail is below if you do.

{% include "partials/_deep_dive.html" with label="What flipping access control on actually does" body="

The switch is a single setting (ACCESS_CONTROL_ENFORCED). While it’s off, public sign-up is open and the setup wizard is reachable — the right state for a demo anyone should be able to explore.

Turning it on closes public sign-up and limits the platform to provisioned users: new accounts must be created and verified by an administrator rather than self-registered, and the open setup paths close. This is the go-live cutover — the deliberate step from “open demonstration” to “the agency’s working system.” It’s held until the demonstration has survived real-world evaluation, because once it’s on, the easy open-door access that makes the demo useful goes away by design.
