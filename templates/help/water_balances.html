The short version

A water balance answers a deceptively hard question: who used what? — in a place where almost nobody is metering. Out of everything a field involves, there is exactly one quantity we can measure for every field, from space, whether its water came from a canal, a well, or rain: the water the crops actually drank. A small, explainable gap is the goal; a perfect zero would be the surprise.

Use vs. supply

The whole model rests on one distinction.

The use measured

The water the crops consumed — evapotranspiration (ET), read from satellites. It’s the demand side, and the one number we can get for every field without a meter. It’s also the floor: it counts only what was consumed, not what leaked from a canal or drained past the roots.

The supplies matched against it

Everything that could have covered that use:

  • Surface water delivered through canals
  • Groundwater pumped from wells
  • Rainfall that fell on the field

A balance lays the supplies alongside the use and asks whether they line up. Because ET is the floor, the supplies delivered usually run a little higher than the ET measured — and that difference is itself information. Reading it honestly is the next section.

Reconciling to a closing balance

Reconciling is just supplies minus use. Start with the measured use, subtract the supplies we already know about — surface deliveries first, then rain — and look at what’s left unexplained. What that leftover means depends on the field.

The rule that keeps it honest

An unexplained leftover is booked as groundwater only where a well actually exists. On a field with no well, that same leftover is unmet demand — the crop wanted more than it got — or a surface number that’s off. It is never phantom pumping. The platform refuses to invent groundwater out of a missing meter.

And expect a gap. ET is the floor, so the water delivered will usually exceed the water consumed — some seeps from canals on the way (conveyance loss), some runs off, some sinks past the roots back into the aquifer. None of that is a failure of the accounting; it’s how irrigation actually behaves. The job isn’t to force the balance to zero — it’s to make the remaining gap small enough to explain.

When the platform has to pick a number, it follows a strict priority ladder — measurement first, estimate last:

  1. A per-field direct measurement wins over everything. If someone metered this field, that is the truth.
  2. A district’s hand-set value is next — an agency’s own recorded figure beats any estimate.
  3. Satellite ET fills the gap only when nothing was measured or entered.
  4. An even split is the last resort, used only when there is no better signal at all.

This ladder also governs the hardest part of the whole problem: a water district holds one water right but delivers unmetered canal water to many parcels — growing different crops with different thirsts. You cannot split that delivery equally, because a vineyard and an alfalfa field next door do not drink the same amount. The platform allocates the district’s delivery to each parcel in proportion to that parcel’s measured ET demand, so the thirsty crop draws the larger share. Splitting evenly would credit water to fields that never used it and starve the ones that did.

Why ET, why satellites

All of this exists to solve one stubborn problem: most water diversions in California are not metered. Reporting is sparse, self-reported, and exempt below certain thresholds — so for the vast majority of fields, nobody knows how much water actually moved.

Evapotranspiration measured from space is the answer to that missing-meter problem. OpenET — a public-private effort led by NASA, the Desert Research Institute, the Environmental Defense Fund, and Google Earth Engine — turns satellite imagery into a consumptive-use figure for any field, no meter required. That is what makes a credible balance possible where one was never possible before.
