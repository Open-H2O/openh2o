The short version

A water balance answers a deceptively hard question: who used what? — in a place where almost nobody is metering. Out of everything a field involves, there is exactly one quantity we can measure for every field, from space, whether its water came from a canal, a well, or rain: the water the crops actually drank. That measured amount — evapotranspiration Evapotranspiration (ET)The water a field consumes — taken up by plants and evaporated from soil. Satellites measure it directly for any field, which is why it works where meters don’t. is the anchor. Everything else — surface deliveries, groundwater pumping, rainfall — is a supply we line up against that anchor to see whether it adds up.

Use vs. supply

The whole model rests on one distinction. The use is the water the crops consumed — the measured evapotranspiration (ET). It’s the demand side, and satellites give it to us directly.

The supplies are everything that could have covered that use: surface water delivered through canals, groundwater pumped from wells, and rainfall that fell on the field. A water balance simply lays the supplies alongside the use and asks whether they match. If a field drank 100 acre-feet and we can account for 100 acre-feet of supply, the books close. When they don’t match, the gap is telling us something — and the next section is about reading that gap honestly.

Reconciling to a closing balance

Reconciling is just supplies minus use. Start with the measured use, subtract the supplies we already know about — surface deliveries first, then rain — and look at what’s left unexplained.

What that leftover means depends on the field. Where a well exists, the unexplained use is almost certainly groundwater the farmer pumped but nobody metered — so the platform books it as groundwater. But where there is no well, that same leftover is not phantom pumping: it’s either unmet demand (the crop wanted more water than it got) or a surface number that’s off. The platform refuses to invent groundwater out of a missing meter. That single rule — residual becomes groundwater only where a well actually exists — is what keeps the numbers honest.

When the platform has to pick a number, it follows a strict priority ladder — measurement first, estimate last:

  1. A per-field direct measurement wins over everything. If someone metered this field, that’s the truth.
  2. A district’s hand-set value is next — an agency’s own recorded figure beats any estimate.
  3. Satellite ET fills the gap only when nothing was measured or entered.
  4. An even split is the last resort, used only when there is no better signal at all.

This ladder also governs the hardest part of the whole problem: a water district holds one water right but delivers unmetered canal water to many parcels — growing different crops with different thirsts. You cannot split that delivery equally, because a vineyard and an alfalfa field next door do not drink the same amount. The platform allocates the district’s delivery to each parcel in proportion to that parcel’s measured ET demand, so the thirsty crop draws the larger share. Splitting evenly would credit water to fields that never used it and starve the ones that did.

Why ET, why satellites

All of this exists to solve one stubborn problem: most water diversions in California are not metered. Reporting is sparse, self-reported, and exempt below certain thresholds — so for the vast majority of fields, nobody knows how much water actually moved.

Evapotranspiration measured from space is the answer to that missing-meter problem. OpenET — built by NASA, the Desert Research Institute (DRI), the Environmental Defense Fund (EDF), and Google — turns satellite imagery into a consumptive-use figure for any field, no meter required. That’s what makes a credible balance possible where one was never possible before. For the precise definitions of these terms, see the Glossary — including OpenET, ET, and Use Area.
