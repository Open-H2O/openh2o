Start with the concept

Before the steps below, it helps to know what the platform is actually computing. It measures the water your crops consumed — from satellites — and reconciles that against the water that supplied them: surface deliveries, groundwater, and rain. A few minutes on the concept makes every step that follows click into place.

Read: How Water Balances Work →

The fast way: the Setup Wizard

You don’t have to do the import steps below by hand. Give the Setup Wizard your district boundary and it populates your use areas, wells, recharge basins, and nearby monitoring stations for you — that’s Steps 1, 2, 8, and 9. What it can’t do is the accounting setup, because those are your agency’s policy choices: water accounts, your water year, management zones, allocation ceilings, and your ledger data (Steps 3 through 7). So run the wizard first, then come back and work the accounting steps. Read this page as the manual route, or as a map of what the wizard already did and what’s left.

Run the Setup Wizard →
Step 1 · the Setup Wizard can do this for you

Import Your Use Areas

Upload use area boundaries from your county GIS department as GeoJSON or Shapefile. Navigate to Use Areas and use the import command. Each use area needs an Assessor Parcel Number (APN).

Go to Use Areas →
Step 2 · the Setup Wizard can do this for you

Import Your Wells

Upload well locations from CSV or Shapefile. Each well needs coordinates and a state well number or local ID. Wells appear on the map and can be linked to use areas.

Go to Wells →
Step 3

Set Up Water Accounts

Create water accounts to group use areas for tracking. Go to Administration > Accounts and create accounts for each management unit (farm, ranch, district zone).

Go to Water Accounts →
Step 4

Create a Water Year

Define the time window for accounting. Go to Compliance > Water Years. Most agencies use the water year (October 1 through September 30).

Go to Water Years →
Step 5

Define Management Zones

A management zone is a sub-area of your district that carries its own allocation — each use area belongs to a zone. Zones are a prerequisite for the next step: an allocation is set per zone, so at least one zone must exist before you can allocate water. Go to Administration > Zones and create your zones, then assign use areas to each.

Go to Zones →
Step 6

Set Allocation Ceilings

An allocation ceiling is the total volume assigned to a zone for a reporting period — the policy ceiling for the whole area. The platform then divides it into per-account allocations, pro-rated by how many use areas each account holds in the zone. Set a ceiling for each zone, water type, and period. New to the difference between a ceiling and an account’s allocation? See the short guide linked at the bottom of this page.

Go to Allocations →
Step 7

Enter Use Ledger Data

Record water supply (positive) and usage (negative) for each use area. Enter manually or import via CSV. Usage can also be derived from satellite evapotranspiration (ET) — how that calculation works is configured on your Methodology page. The use ledger is the core record of your water data.

Go to Use Ledger → | View the Methodology →
Step 8 · the Setup Wizard can do this for you

Connect Monitoring Stations

Link external data sources (CDEC stream gauges, USGS wells, CIMIS weather stations) to automatically pull measurements. Go to Monitoring Stations > Add Station.

Go to Monitoring Stations →
Step 9 · the Setup Wizard imports recharge basins for you

Add Surface Diversions and Recharge Areas

Record surface water rights with their points of diversion. Track managed aquifer recharge events at spreading basins or injection wells.

Go to Surface Diversions → | Go to Recharge Areas →
Step 10

Generate State Reports

When your reporting period is complete, generate GEARS CSV (per-well or by-ET extraction) and CalWATRS CSV (surface diversions) for submission to the Water Board. Go to Reports > Generate.

Go to Reports →

Setting up surface deliveries?

Two agency-wide settings shape how the platform counts canal deliveries: how much of a delivery the crop actually uses, and what happens to a district’s unused water at year-end. This guide explains both in plain language and shows how one district can differ from the agency default.
