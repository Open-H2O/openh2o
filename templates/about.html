OpenH2O

Open-source water-data management for California

OpenH2O is an open-source water-data management platform built for Groundwater Sustainability Agencies and water districts. It brings an agency's water data — measurements, deliveries, wells, surface diversions, managed aquifer recharge, and mixed-use accounting — into a single system the agency owns and understands. The entire platform runs on a $15-a-month virtual private server and can be deployed by an AI coding assistant pointed at this repository.

Standing on the Groundwater Accounting Platform

OpenH2O exists because the Groundwater Accounting Platform (GAP) proved something that was far from obvious when it started: that a partnership across public agencies, a nonprofit, and private firms could build and sustain shared, open-source infrastructure for water management — and hold it to a standard that raised the bar for every tool in the field.

GAP was originated in 2018 by the Environmental Defense Fund with the Rosedale-Rio Bravo Water Storage District, engineered by Sitka Technology Group — now part of Environmental Science Associates (ESA) — and opened and scaled to agencies statewide by the California Water Data Consortium. Years of that work are the example OpenH2O learned from.

GAP also made the hard part clear. Standing up the software is the easy half; the decisive work is everything around it — training, outreach, and the patient support that turns a tool into a system an agency actually trusts. OpenH2O treats that human and institutional work as the real work, not an afterthought.

Where GAP focused, by design, on groundwater and SGMA, OpenH2O extends the same open, partnership-minded approach across all of water — surface water, groundwater, and the reporting the state requires. OpenH2O is an independent reimplementation on an open stack, indebted to GAP's example and glad to keep learning from the people who built it.

Policy Timeline

2014
SGMA signed

The Sustainable Groundwater Management Act requires roughly 200 new local agencies to bring 94 critically overdrafted basins into balance

2016
AB 1755 passed

The Open and Transparent Water Data Act directs the state to make its water data public and interoperable

2019
Water Data Consortium founded

An independent nonprofit forms to carry out AB 1755 and steward California's open-source water data tools

2021
Accounting platform made available

Proven first at the Rosedale-Rio Bravo Water Storage District and in Nebraska, the Groundwater Accounting Platform is made available to California agencies through a partnership of the Consortium, the Department of Water Resources, the State Water Board, and the Environmental Defense Fund

2024
OpenET operational

Satellite evapotranspiration data becomes production-ready as a district data feed

2025
Source code published

The Environmental Defense Fund and Environmental Science Associates publish the platform's full source code on GitHub (the Qanat project), so any agency can download and run it

2026
OpenH2O

OpenH2O extends the open accounting model beyond groundwater — adding surface water, managed aquifer recharge, live OpenET integration, and California reporting formats — on a fully open stack an agency can host itself

Credits

Open water data

California Water Data Consortium

The independent nonprofit founded in 2019 to carry out California's Open and Transparent Water Data Act (AB 1755) — the neutral table where state agencies, local agencies, Tribes, academia, and NGOs work to make the state's water data open and interoperable.

The open-source Groundwater Accounting Platform whose methodology OpenH2O independently reimplements was originated by the Environmental Defense Fund with the Rosedale-Rio Bravo Water Storage District, engineered by Sitka Technology Group (now ESA), then opened and scaled statewide by the Consortium.

Proven in production

Pioneering Implementations

Rosedale-Rio Bravo Water Storage District (California), and Natural Resources Districts in Nebraska

Local agencies that proved a water-data platform can be built and run in-house. OpenH2O independently reimplements that approach so any agency can do the same.

Prior art

Open-Source Foundations

Environmental Science Associates (formerly Sitka Technology Group) — the open-source Qanat platform

Community-licensed (AGPL) codebases studied as prior art. OpenH2O's design most closely parallels the current Qanat platform; its methodology was independently reimplemented in a different language and stack, with none of their source code copied.
